Okay, I’ll admit it: I used to think robot vacuums were just glorified cat toys.

After my ancient Roomba 600 series smeared dog poop across the living room carpet for the third time, I swore off them. My wife still brings up "The Incident." So when the iRobot Roomba j7+ showed up with its P.O.O.P. guarantee, I was skeptical but desperate. My dog is a fluffy disaster machine, and my floors were paying the price.

First Impressions & Setup

Right out of the box, the j7+ felt like a premium upgrade. The Clean Base dock is surprisingly compact — not the spaceship I expected. Setup took maybe 10 minutes. The app walked me through connecting to Wi-Fi, and then the robot immediately started mapping. No boundary markers needed. No babysitting. It just… learned.

Honestly, the mapping is half the fun. I named the rooms ("Crumb Central," "Dog Fur Alley"), and within a few runs, it knew the layout better than I do. The build quality feels solid, no creaky plastic, and the self-emptying base seals well enough that I don’t smell last week’s dust bunnies every time I walk by.

Performance & Real-World Use

Here’s the thing: the obstacle avoidance is witchcraft-level good. I scattered charging cables, shoes, and a couple of dog toys just to test it. The iRobot Roomba j7+ gently nudged nothing, avoided everything, and even sent me photos of “unexpected obstacles” with a polite “Is this okay?” prompt. One morning it detected a freshly regurgitated sock (thanks, pup) and simply… steered clear. No smearing. No horror story. The P.O.O.P. guarantee isn’t a gimmick — it’s a marriage saver.

The suction is indeed 10x stronger than my old 600 series. It picks up kibble crumbs, cat litter, and the hair tumbleweeds that accumulate under my couch. With the self-emptying dock, I forget the bin exists for about 60 days. The bag seals itself when full, so you’re not inhaling a dust mushroom. Mapping gets smarter every run — it now knows that the kitchen needs extra attention after dinner. I tell Alexa “clean the kitchen,” and it rolls out like a tiny, obedient butler.

The Downsides

Not gonna lie: the bag situation stings. A 3-pack of replacement bags runs around $20, which means you’re dropping about $80 a year if you use them as recommended. For a $500 vacuum, that feels like a subscription fee I didn’t sign up for. Some users have hacked reusable bags, but that’s messy and voids the warranty.

The other elephant in the room: no mopping. Zero. Zilch. At this price point, competitors like Roborock toss in a basic mop pad or even vibrating water tanks. The j7+ is strictly a dry-floor kind of friend. If you’ve got hard floors and want that two-in-one magic, this ain’t it.

Pros & Cons

Pros

  • Stupid-good obstacle avoidance — genuinely avoids cables and pet messes
  • Self-emptying dock lasts up to 60 days, no contact with dust
  • Mapping improves over time, and room-specific cleaning works flawlessly
  • Alexa/Google voice commands are responsive, no remapping needed
  • 10x suction vs old Roombas is a quiet, efficient upgrade

Cons

  • Expensive proprietary bags add an ongoing cost (~$80/year)
  • No mopping at all — feels missing for a $500 vacuum
  • Battery life (90 min) may require recharge for very large homes
  • Dustbin could be bigger (400 ml) but self-emptying makes it moot

Final Verdict

Who’s this for? Pet owners who’ve lived through the horror of a poop-smearing robot. Anyone who values smart mapping, genuine obstacle avoidance, and the glorious laziness of a self-emptying bin. If you’ve got mostly carpet or rugs and just need a vacuum that won’t eat your phone charger, the iRobot Roomba j7+ is a no-brainer.

Who should avoid it? If you’ve got hard floors and want mopping, or if you’re on a tight budget and the bag costs make you twitchy, look elsewhere. For everyone else, this is the closest I’ve come to trusting a machine with my floors — and my dignity.
