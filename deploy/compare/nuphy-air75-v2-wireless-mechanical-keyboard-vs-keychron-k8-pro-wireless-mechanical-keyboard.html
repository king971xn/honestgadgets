NuPhy Air75 V2 Wireless Mechanical Keyboard Deep Dive

I’ll be honest — the first time I saw a low-profile mechanical keyboard, I thought it was a gimmick. But after using the NuPhy Air75 V2, I get why folks rave about it. The low-profile Gateron Browns make typing feel almost laptop-like in travel but with that satisfying tactile bump. One reviewer mentioned their wrists stopped hurting after switching from a traditional high-profile board, and I totally buy it. The 75% layout saves a ton of desk space, and it looks sleek as hell in that aluminum frame.

The connectivity is where this thing really shines. That 2.4GHz dongle gives you lag-free gaming performance — you won’t notice any difference from a wired keyboard. And switching between devices? You just tap a key combo and you’re typing on your iPad or work laptop instantly. The 4000mAh battery keeps it alive for two weeks even with RGB on, which feels generous.

Where it falls short: Here’s the kicker — those keycaps are proprietary. You can’t slap on any random set of MX keycaps you’ve been eyeing on Drop. NuPhy sells replacements, but they’re pricier and the selection is tiny. Also, the customization software is Windows-only, so Mac users are stuck with the default lighting effects. If you love tweaking every single LED, this might drive you nuts.

Keychron K8 Pro Wireless Mechanical Keyboard Deep Dive

Keychron’s K8 Pro is the keyboard that almost makes me believe in love at first type. The Gateron G Pro Browns have that perfect tactile bump — not mushy, not clicky — and the factory pre-lubed stabilizers mean there’s zero rattle. I’ve typed on $200+ boards that didn’t sound this good out of the box. The TKL layout gives you dedicated function keys and arrow keys, which is a lifesaver if you work in spreadsheets or do any kind of dev work.

The hot-swappable sockets let you try out any standard MX switch you want, which is huge for tinkerers. And the VIA/QMK support? That’s game-changing if you want to remap keys without downloading some dodgy software. The built-in Mac/Windows switch on the side is something more boards should steal — it just works.

Where it stumbles: Multiple owners complain about random Bluetooth dropouts. Losing connection for 3-5 seconds while you’re in flow state is like someone yanking the steering wheel. Some have resorted to using it wired full-time, which defeats the purpose. The RGB is also dimmer than a candle in a bright room — you’ll barely see it during the day. And Keychron’s customer support? Let’s just say you don’t want to be the one emailing them about a loose USB-C port two months in.

Ready to pull the trigger? Both are solid, but one might fit you better.

Real-World Performance

Look, specs only tell half the story. How do these actually hold up in your daily grind?

Office & Coding: Both are great, but the Keychron K8 Pro’s standard profile and full MX compatibility mean you can fine-tune the feel with different switches if the Browns aren’t your thing. The NuPhy Air75 V2, though, is a wrist-saver if you’re typing 12+ hours — that low height reduces strain noticeably. For loud open offices, the NuPhy’s lower profile switches are also a bit quieter.

Gaming: NuPhy Air75 V2 wins hands down. That 2.4GHz dongle gives you zero perceptible latency, while the Keychron relies on Bluetooth (which can be iffy) or wired. If you’re playing competitive shooters, you don’t want to gamble on a Bluetooth hiccup.

Travel & Portability: The NuPhy is slimmer and lighter, and the 75% layout is more compact. It slides into a backpack like a dream. The Keychron K8 Pro is a chunky TKL — still portable, but noticeably bulkier.

Multi-Device Juggling: Both handle three Bluetooth devices with easy switching. The NuPhy adds a 2.4GHz option, so you can have your gaming PC on the dongle and two other devices on Bluetooth. That’s slick if you bounce between machines constantly.

The Hidden Costs

Nobody talks about these until it’s too late. Pay attention.

NuPhy Air75 V2: That proprietary keycap stems mean you’re locked into NuPhy’s ecosystem. Want a fresh PBT set in a cool colorway? You’ll pay a premium and choice is limited. Plus, if you’re on a Mac, you can’t change the RGB patterns at all — you’re stuck with whatever’s pre-loaded. That’s a bummer for a keyboard that markets itself as Mac-compatible.

Keychron K8 Pro: The stock keycaps get shiny and greasy within weeks. Factor in $30-$50 for a decent aftermarket set. The Bluetooth unreliability might force you to use it wired, so you’re essentially overpaying for wireless you can’t trust. And if something breaks after the return window, Keychron’s support might leave you footing the shipping bill and tearing your hair out.

Final Verdict

I’m not gonna pretend one keyboard rules them all. It depends on what kind of nerd you are.
